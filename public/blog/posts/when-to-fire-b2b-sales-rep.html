Revenue has stalled. The board is pressing for answers. Your instinct says replace the rep. But before making that decision ‚Äî which costs $100K to $150K between recruiting, training, and lost deals ‚Äî there's a more important question: is the problem actually the rep?

At post-Series B startups, growth pressure turns diagnosis into reflex. The rep missed quota? Replace them. But in most cases I see while structuring revenue operations, the rep is the last link in a chain with far deeper problems: unclear value proposition, misaligned pricing, wrong target segment, or poorly qualified demand.

This article is an elimination framework. We'll walk through each layer ‚Äî from product to rep ‚Äî so you make the right decision with data, not with pressure.

The most expensive decision in sales isn't firing too late. It's firing without knowing if the problem was actually the rep.

Layer 1: Is the Value Proposition Clear?

This is the starting point because it contaminates everything else. If the market doesn't understand why they should buy from you, the best rep in the world won't fix it.

The symptoms are subtle: the team books meetings but can't get past the first call. Prospects say "interesting, we'll evaluate" and vanish. Or worse ‚Äî the rep has to reinvent the pitch for every meeting because there's no consistent narrative.

30s
The elevator test Ask 3 different reps to explain what you do in 30 seconds. If the answers diverge significantly, the value proposition isn't clear ‚Äî the problem isn't the rep.

How to rule it out

Review recordings from the last 10 sales meetings. If the rep spends more than 5 minutes explaining what the product does (instead of the problem it solves), the proposition needs work. If loss reasons include phrases like "didn't understand the value" or "thought it was something else," the gap is here.

We wrote in detail about how to diagnose and fix this in our article Unclear Value Proposition: The Silent Growth Killer.

‚úÖ Ruled out if:

The team articulates the proposition consistently, prospects quickly understand the value, and loss reasons don't include confusion about what you do. Move to the next layer.

Layer 2: Is the Pricing Right?

The second most common blind spot. The value proposition may be perfect, but if pricing is misaligned with buyer perception, deals will stall ‚Äî especially at the proposal stage.

There are two opposite mistakes. Pricing too low creates suspicion: "if it costs so little, does it actually work?" Pricing too high without justification leads to committee paralysis ‚Äî the internal champion can't defend the investment.

70%
Deals stalling at proposal If more than 70% of deals die between proposal presentation and close, the most likely signal is pricing misalignment with perceived value ‚Äî not a rep failing at negotiation.

How to rule it out

Analyze where deals die in the funnel. If conversion is healthy up to the proposal stage and drops off after, the problem is likely pricing or packaging. Cross-reference with logged objections: "too expensive," "need approval," and "we'll reevaluate next quarter" are classic misalignment symptoms.

‚úÖ Ruled out if:

Proposal-to-close conversion is above 25-30%, pricing objections are occasional (not systemic), and customers who buy acknowledge the value for what they pay. Move on.

Layer 3: Are the Segment and Customer Profile Right?

Clear proposition, fair pricing ‚Äî but selling to people who don't need what you offer. This is more common than it seems, especially post-Series B, when volume pressure pushes the team into adjacent segments where the product doesn't have the same fit.

The classic symptom: sales cycles drag on, prospects ask for customizations outside the roadmap, and customers who close churn quickly. The rep is working hard but swimming against the current.

Signal Indicates
Sales cycle 2-3x longer than expected Selling to a profile without urgency or natural fit
High churn in the first 6 months Customers bought but the product doesn't solve their problem
Constant requests for features outside the roadmap The segment needs something different from what you built
Rep needs aggressive discounts to close Perceived value for the segment is lower than the price

How to rule it out

Compare performance by segment. If one vertical or company profile converts 3x better than another, the problem isn't the rep covering the weaker segment ‚Äî it's territory allocation. Also analyze your best customers (those who renew, expand, and refer). Is the team trying to sell to more companies like them, or has it dispersed to different profiles?

‚úÖ Ruled out if:

The ideal customer profile is validated, territories are comparable across reps, and early churn is below 15%. Move on.

Layer 4: Is Demand Generation Working?

Everything above can be perfect ‚Äî clear proposition, aligned pricing, validated segment ‚Äî and the rep still underperforms because they're receiving low-quality leads or insufficient volume.

In many startups, the qualification handoff between marketing and sales has gaps. Leads are passed as "qualified" without consistent criteria. The rep spends 60% of their time in meetings that will never become deals, and the remaining 40% doesn't generate enough volume to hit quota.

3x
Minimum pipeline coverage The rep needs at least 3x their quota in qualified pipeline to have a real chance of hitting target. If coverage is below that, the problem is generation ‚Äî not execution. Read more about calculating coverage in our article on the math behind sales quotas.

How to rule it out

Measure conversion rate by lead source. If referral leads convert at 40% but inbound leads convert at 5%, the generation channel is delivering volume without quality. Also check: how many meetings does the rep need to generate one qualified opportunity? If it's more than 8-10, the inbound funnel needs adjustment before demanding results from the rep.

‚úÖ Ruled out if:

The rep has 3x or more coverage, leads are qualified by consistent criteria, and opportunity volume is comparable to other reps. Now ‚Äî evaluate the individual.

Layer 5: It Is the Rep ‚Äî Now What?

You've worked through the four previous layers. The proposition is clear, pricing makes sense, the segment is right, and demand arrives qualified. But this specific rep isn't converting. Now the analysis is about the individual.

Diagnosing the rep without first ruling out the system is like prescribing medication without running tests. You might get lucky ‚Äî but you'll probably get it wrong, and the cost of being wrong is high.

The 4 Types of Gap

Type Symptoms What to Do
Skill High activity, low conversion. Books meetings but doesn't advance deals. Loses at negotiation or proposal stage. Trainable. Intensive coaching for 60-90 days with a senior rep.
Will Low activity, empty pipeline. Avoids prospecting. Doesn't follow up. Shows no urgency. Direct conversation. 60-day improvement plan. If no change in 30 days, it likely won't change.
Fit Performed well in a different context. Reasonable activity, but profile doesn't match the segment. E.g.: enterprise rep selling to SMBs. Reposition internally if possible. If not, respectful separation.
Context Performance dropped after a change (new territory, new product, new manager). Previously performed well. Restore conditions or allow adaptation time. Not a separation candidate ‚Äî yet.
üí° The most common gap is the most treatable

Skill gap is the most frequent and the most responsive to structured training. Studies show that consistent individual coaching generates 19-32% improvement in conversion rates. Before giving up on this rep, ask yourself: did we offer real coaching, or just pressure for results?

The Metrics That Matter

Don't base the decision on quota attainment alone. Compare these metrics for the rep in question against the rest of the team:

  • Activity: Volume and consistency of calls, emails, meetings booked
  • Pipeline generated: Volume and quality of deals created
  • Stage conversion rate: Where exactly do deals die? First meeting? Proposal? Negotiation?
  • Cycle velocity: How long from first contact to close?
  • Average deal size: Are they closing smaller contracts than the team?
  • Forecast accuracy: Does what they project actually materialize?

A rep with high activity and strong pipeline but low conversion has a skill gap. A rep with low activity and an empty pipeline has a will gap. The response is radically different.

The Improvement Plan That Actually Works

Improvement plans have a terrible reputation ‚Äî and for good reason. At most companies, they're pre-termination formalities with unrealistic goals and no real support. A well-designed plan is different: it's a genuine diagnostic and development tool.

Element Effective plan ‚úÖ Performative plan ‚ùå
Duration 60-90 days (compatible with the sales cycle) 30 days (impossible in complex sales)
Goals Leading indicators (activity, pipeline, meetings) + outcomes Only final quota ("close $500K in 30 days")
Support Weekly coaching, joint work with senior rep None ‚Äî "figure it out"
Tone Collaborative ("let's solve this together") Punitive ("improve or leave")
Consequences Clear from day one, no surprises Vague or inconsistent

Weeks 1-2: Joint diagnosis. Identify specific gaps. Define 3-5 improvement metrics (e.g.: increase qualified meetings from 4 to 8/month, improve first-meeting-to-proposal conversion from 30% to 45%). Align support resources.

Weeks 3-4: Execution with intensive coaching. Weekly check-in. Joint calls with a senior rep on at least 3 meetings. Pipeline review with manager.

Weeks 5-6: Midpoint assessment. Is there progress? If no improvement in leading indicators ‚Äî honest conversation about next steps.

Weeks 7-8: Final assessment. Have metrics improved? If yes, transition to ongoing coaching. If not, separation with respect and clarity.

Clear Signals: Invest or Let Go

Even with the framework, sometimes the answer is more straightforward than it appears. Here are the signals that separate the two decisions.

üî¥ Time to let go

  • Zero motivation: Activity doesn't improve after direct, repeated feedback
  • Negative team impact: Complains publicly, sabotages processes, poisons culture
  • Never takes ownership: There's always an external excuse for every missed target
  • Inflated pipeline: Projects deals that will never close to appear productive
  • Resistant to feedback: Doesn't implement guidance. Keeps the same patterns
  • 3+ consecutive quarters below 50% of quota with adequate support

üü¢ Time to invest

  • Leading indicators improving: Activity and pipeline growing, even if revenue hasn't yet
  • Receptive to feedback: Actively implements guidance, asks questions, practices, adjusts
  • Recent context change: Territory, product, or management changed. Needs time
  • Track record of results: Performed well before. Something specific changed
  • Contribution beyond sales: Even without hitting quota, adds value through culture, mentoring, or process
  • Deals in pipeline with real progress: Not volume ‚Äî quality and advancement

If the Decision Is to Let Go: How to Handle It

When the framework and improvement plan confirm it's time, how you handle the process matters as much as the decision itself.

  1. Before the conversation: Prepare all documentation, calculate severance, set the date. Align with legal. Have a ready plan for transitioning active deals.
  2. The conversation: Be direct, respectful, and brief. This isn't the time to relitigate performance. The rep shouldn't be caught off guard if the process was well-conducted.
  3. Pipeline transition: Assign an owner for each deal. Proactively communicate with clients. Complete the transition within 48 hours.
  4. Team communication: Be honest without exposing details. Don't badmouth, don't reveal individual performance. Focus on the transition plan.
48h
Critical pipeline window Deals without an active owner for more than 48 hours after a rep's departure have 60% lower close probability. Have the transition plan ready before the separation conversation.

Conclusion

Most founders I work with arrive with the same frustration: "my rep isn't performing." In more than half of cases, after working through the diagnostic funnel, the problem wasn't the rep ‚Äî it was an unclear value proposition, misaligned pricing, a poorly chosen segment, or insufficient demand.

That doesn't mean it's never the rep. Sometimes it is. But the framework ensures that when you make the decision ‚Äî whether to invest in coaching, reposition, or let go ‚Äî it's based on evidence, preceded by real diagnosis, and executed with respect for the professional and the operation.

The most expensive mistake isn't the decision itself. It's making the decision without knowing what's actually causing the problem.
